When Better Fraud Detection
Becomes Less Explainable

This research shows that common imbalance-handling strategies in deep generative fraud detection models can improve detection performance while systematically destroying explainability.

The failure does not originate in explainability methods such as SHAP or attention. It originates earlier — in the geometry of learned latent representations.

The Central Paradox

In highly imbalanced domains (0.17% fraud prevalence), practitioners routinely introduce imbalance-aware objectives. Metrics such as AUPRC improve, and models appear operationally stronger.

However, as imbalance-aware generative models perform better, their internal representations collapse — making explanations increasingly unreliable.

Figure 1: Ratio-weighting acts as a signal amplifier that induces clipping.

What Goes Wrong: The "Amplifier" Effect

  1. The Problem: 499 citizens vs 1 criminal. Standard AI ignores the criminal to score 99.8% accuracy.
  2. The SOTA Fix: Give the criminal a 500x Signal Amplifier (Ratio-weighting). The model must identify them.
  3. The Mechanism: This scaling amplifies gradient variance acting on latent variance parameters ($\sigma^2$).
  4. The Consequence: The minority manifold collapses into a low-rank, near-singular structure (a dot).

We catch the criminal (High Recall), but the distortion destroys the forensic evidence (Low Explainability).

The Solution: Mahalanobis-Balanced VAE

Rather than amplifying gradients (screaming), we introduce a geometry-aware regularisation strategy. We constrain minority latents via their Mahalanobis distance to the majority manifold.

Decoupled Magnitude

Regularisation strength $\lambda$ is not scaled by the imbalance ratio $r$, preventing variance explosion.
